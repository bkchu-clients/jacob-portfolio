Southwest Airlines, Cargo

Transforming a digital experience

Southwest Cargo provides fast and efficient shipping services for valued customers around the country. As a subsidiary of Southwest Airlines, Southwest Cargo focuses on enhancing customer experience with every package that is shipped.

My team consisted of 6 members. Two designers, one project manager and three UI developers. We collaborated with the client’s team to finish this project in 6 design sprints.

The Challenge: How do we redesign Southwest’s Cargo website to keep it competitive with other airlines?

The Solution: We created a responsive web-based experience that emulates Southwest’s digital branding to allow for smooth transitions into next generation designs.

My Role: Along with my Experience Director, Lan Nguyen, I was the experience designer who focused on creating high fidelity wireframes, IA documentation and design patterns.

The Process

1. Sprint! (2 week intervals)

We began each sprint by prioritizing features. Once we figured out which features were high-priority I began designing wireframes.

2. Design Review

We presented our designs to our main stakeholders on a weekly basis to receive feedback as well as approvals.

3. Feedback

In addition to presenting designs to stakeholders, they had the opportunity to look through WIP designs using InVision.

4. Iterate

Based on feedback, the experience director and I would iterate on our designs and prepare for our sprint review.

5. Demo to Client

I presented current designs to key stakeholders to receive final feedback before moving onto the next sprint.

Information Architecture

Before redesigning Southwest Cargo we had to understand how each page interacted with one another. A firm understanding of the taxonomy would solidify how we would organize “parent” pages, nested pages and external links. After mapping out the current state of Southwest Cargo, our experience director and I went to work. We created a sitemap that efficiently showed how each page interacted within the website while showing which pages could link to another page.

Prioritizing User Flow

During sprint planning we had to understand which tasks were the most common and frequent. After talking to our stakeholders and reviewing what the main purpose of the website was we determined a couple high priority user flows. We learned that users go to the Cargo website to locate their nearest shipping facility as well as track shipments.

As a user, I want to learn about each facility so I can visit and send my cargo from the nearest location.

As a user, I want to see my tracking history so I can locate my shipment.

Conceptualizing Designs

The revamped Southwest Cargo website allows customers to seamlessly navigate through the site no matter what device they’re using. We followed Southwest’s brand philosophy and kept the user at the focus when making design decisions. Our goal was to give enough affordance throughout our designs to make it easy for users to locate, ship, learn, and manage shipments.

Next Steps

We finished the design sprints in 3 months. After multiple iterations, we redesigned Southwest Cargo’s responsive website. In order to allow for next generation adjustments, we created a framework that could be applied to current and future designs. The website is now live but we are still in the process of updating UI based on client feedback.

Research, research, and more research
Research is not just valuable in the early stages of a project but valuable at every stage in the project cycle. As more people start to use Cargo’s new website it is essential to understand how they’re using the product and what features to add.
