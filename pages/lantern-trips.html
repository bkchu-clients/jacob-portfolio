Lantern Trips

Group travel made simple

Lantern Trips is a mobile application that aims to simplify trip planning and become the most trusted source for travel. Although Lantern Trips has conducted market research, defined features, and identified target users, they wish to assess and iterate on what exists in the market so they can deliver a competitive app.

The Challenge: How do we design for collaborative travel during a trip where everyone can share and contribute?

The Solution: We designed a mobile app that allows travelers to create an itinerary, collaborate on activities and share their moments on social media.

My Role: UX Designer, Researcher, Project Manager

How do we make Lantern Trips stand out from their competitors? We began defining areas that could be improved on while integrating Lantern Trips core values:

To cultivate a shared travel community with collaborative trip building.

Create unique travel experiences utilizing trusted recommendations.

Inspire discovery & exploration from travelers like you.

Research

The companies that we thought best represented Lantern Trips values were Bonjournal, TripAdvisor, Tripcast and Trippy. By doing a heuristic evaluation and competitive analysis on current market leaders we realized a few things.

1. Companies that tried to have too many features were not as successful as those who focused on a few core functions.

2. Lantern Trips didn’t have a true direct competitor who tried to solve the hassles of group trip planning.

3. Competitors would have complicated task flows that hindered users from completing simple tasks such as booking a flight.

Interviewing Users -

My team conducted user interviews to empathize with our target audience to find out what aspects of travel inspired them and what pain points they came across. After identifying qualified participants, we ended with a sample pool of 15 people and took their key points and conducted an affinity diagram.

“Being able to share an itinerary and make decisions was a real pain. It was too much information.”

Major Findings -

Pain Points

- Sharing between people within their group (itinerary & photos).

- Planning with people is difficult because it's hard to share between platforms.

- Travelers don't think that they can trust online reviews.

- Travelers feel as though travel blogs are paid advertisement and mask their true feelings.

Needs

- Travelers want to hear their friends and 3rd party opinions.

- Travelers want to share recommendations so that they can inspire others.

- Travelers want to post things that are still fresh in their head.

- Travelers want to get local's advice/tips/suggestions.

Motivations

- Group travel tends to be high cost and therefore content heavy. Users are more likely to post on expensive, high effort travels.

- Being able to go back to your photos/posts.

- Majority of travelers reported being discouraged from posting due to data costs and limited wi-fi access.

For my team, a journey map was a chance to see what opportunities were presented and how we were going to capitalize on them.

Information Architecture -

We organized categories through card sorting. After going through multiple rounds in order to solidify the taxonomy and app structure, we created the sitemap. The information gathered from user interviews were used to construct how the pages on the interface were grouped, what order they appeared in, and the relationship between different elements.

Define & Design

We held a design studio with our client to collaboratively come up with different iterations on prioritized features. After multiple rounds of designing, we were able to focus our solutions on the following topics: Collaborative Planning, Content Sharing and Documenting to Social Media.

By creating a low-fidelity prototype we were able to get feedback on the features we decided were the most practical and important for an MVP. Our team went through multiple rounds of user testing with paper prototypes and medium-fidelity wireframes.

User Feedback -

1. Users complained that the "segment page" was too condensed and that they wanted to view more segment details before clicking "going".
2. Users wished that they could preview the section on their schedule before approving the segment.
3. Users wanted icons on "itinerary page" to be more prominent. The "share" icon caused too much delay in performing main task due to the placement of the button.
4. Users wanted to see an "album block" section on the "segment page" so that they can preview what photos already exist for that section.
5. Users were confused with the CTAs (delete selected photos and cancel) on the "carousel image page".

Usability Tests -

1. There was a lot of visual confusion on the page due to the different icons. We saw that users weren't benefiting from an overview of who was in the photo album, so we changed the naming convention to text.

2. Users were confused that a stack of photos was differentiated by just a number on the photo. We changed the stack to be visually different from a single photo by adding a "stacked" photo effect.

Next Steps

We presented our prototype to our client and showed them the features that would be included in the MVP. Moving forward, we suggested a few things:

After user testing with multiple people, we found that it would be much easier on the client's developer if Lantern Trips was integrated with another app that is already utilized by travelers such as DropBox or Google Photos in order to share/store content. We told them to explore different options like implementing another platform or simplifying the content in order to solve the learnability issue.

We encouraged our client to continue usability testing on the MVP as well as the overall layout of the app with the prototype and the high-fidelity wireframes.

What I Learned

Working with clients won’t always be easy. Luckily, our client was willing to compromise on features to finish the design sprint and focus on what users told us during testing. I had to be able to justify a design change not based on personal bias but from user feedback. As we constantly iterated on our designs I had to learn to accept design changes and not get caught up with just the visuals.
